General Dyer and Rowlt Act

General Dyer

General Reginald Dyer (1864-1927) was a British Army officer best known for his role in the Jallianwala Bagh massacre in 1919, in which British Indian Army troops under his command opened fire on a crowd of unarmed Indian civilians, killing hundreds and injuring thousands. Dyer believed that the use of force was necessary to maintain British control over India and preserve the empire, and he saw the massacre as a necessary response to growing Indian nationalist sentiment and unrest. However, the Jallianwala Bagh massacre was widely condemned both in India and internationally, and Dyer was eventually relieved of his command and forced to retire from the army. He became a controversial figure, with some British conservatives hailing him as a hero and others condemning him as a brutal and unjust oppressor.



Rawlt Act

The Rowlatt Act, also known as the Anarchical and Revolutionary Crimes Act, was a controversial law passed by the British colonial government in India in 1919, in the aftermath of the First World War and the nonviolent civil disobedience movement led by Mahatma Gandhi. The law gave the colonial government sweeping powers to arrest and detain anyone suspected of political dissent without trial and for an indefinite period of time. It also restricted civil liberties, such as freedom of the press and assembly. The Rowlatt Act was met with widespread protests and strikes in India, with many Indians seeing it as a betrayal of the promises of self-rule made by the British during the war. The protests eventually culminated in the Jallianwala Bagh massacre, which further fueled nationalist sentiment and demands for independence. The Rowlatt Act was eventually repealed in 1922, but its legacy remained an important symbol of colonial oppression and resistance in India's struggle for independence.
